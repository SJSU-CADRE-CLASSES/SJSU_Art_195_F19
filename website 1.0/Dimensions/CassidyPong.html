• Cassidy Pong

    Reflected Self

    Medium:

    wood, acrylic sheet, two way mirror film, neodymium magnets

    Dimensions:

    6ft (182.88cm) x 6.5ft (198.12cm)


    Artist Statement

    Reflected Self is an immersive installation featuring two-way mirrors constructed into a multi-layered wall that acts as a commentary on society’s behavior in how we perceive ourselves as individuals and as a community. Increasing popularity of social media and rapid advancements in technology has led to distorted ideas of the “self,” the “individual,” and “society” where these terms have morphed into an array of judgmental and opinionated views of ourselves and each other. Whether it be negative or positive, this installation forces us to become aware of our own bodies as well as our perception of those around us.

    Creation Process

    Personally, I like to explore how the body interacts with an environment and what kind of dialogue it creates with the objects around it. Evolving from a digital space to the physical, I came across the idea of mirrors. Mirrors have always had a dominant factor in our lives consistently present in our bathrooms, living rooms, bedrooms, and hallways. We use them to look at ourselves, our flaws, our imperfections, or to boost our self-confidence and give praise. I was greatly inspired by many artists who have used mirrors in their exhibitions such as Christian Larsen’s Mobile Mirrors, Yayoi Kusama’s infamous Infinity Mirrors Exhibition, and Jeppe Hein’s Mirror Wall and Mirror Labyrinth NY.

    In creating this work, I first thought about utilizing frames and mirrors, situated in a line, as a way to play with the idea of perception in how we view ourselves in comparison to viewing others. However, the idea felt a little simple so the idea evolved into panels of two-way mirrors that would be suspended in a circle: from the outside the mirrors would be reflective, while on the inside of the circle, people can look through the mirrors and seeing everyone around them. This idea eventually evolved into creating a giant, two-way mirror wall.

    The process begins with the creation of the two wood beams that the mirrors are wedged between. After acquiring six planks, I glued them together and sanded them down. After doing so, I spraypainted them black/dark brown so as to make the mirrors stand out, rather than having the reflective mirrors compete with a brighter color of wood.

    The bulk of the project was focused on the "mirrors." I bought different shapes and thickness of acrylic ranging from 22 x 22in to 10 x 10in, and 1/8" thickness to 1/4" thickness. I began by stacking and offsetting the mirrors in front of/behind one another and applied the neodymium magnets to the corners and middle to hold them together. Next, as I gradually built upward from the sides, I slid the mirrors into the the wood beams to hold the structure up. After figuring out the positioning of the acrylic sheet, I applied two-way mirror film to each individual mirror -- making sure that the reflective and transparent sides are all lined up with its respected side.

    Unfortunately, installing the work proved many difficulties. Over time, the magnets had a hard time holding up the weight of the acrylic and the sheets would fall down. Therefore, I had to utilize wire and gorilla/duct tape as support. In addition, there was a lot of space within the wood beams for the acrylic to shift around, causing the wall to bend and warp. Therefore, I stuffed the acrylic in with foam to keep it from moving and to create a tighter fit. This made the wall more straight and less likely to slide around and fall.
